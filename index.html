To catch up with the World Ocean Day, choose sustainable seafood

Sat, Jan 1, 2022, 0:00 AM

Hong Kong is currently the second largest per capita seafood consumption place in Asia. Each Hong Kong people eat about 72 kilograms of seafood each year, which is an astonishing 3.6 times higher than the global average of 20 kilograms. According to the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization, about 90 percent of the world's fish stocks are fully fished or overfished. As June 8 is the World Ocean Day, Hong Kong people must turn to sustainable seafood, as our choices have a direct and far-reaching impact on the world's fishery resources.

Sustainable seafood is fishing in a way to maintain a healthy population of fish in the ocean, meaning there will be enough seafood for future generations, marine life and its habitats to survive, and those who depend on fishing to continue to make their livings. We need to act now or we may be the last generation to get food from the ocean. While governments and seafood companies ultimately must do their part to protect the oceans, as consumers, we need to demand traceability in our seafood supply chains.

World Wildlife Fund encouraged consumers and suppliers to make responsible choices and take action to conserve marine resources. Last year, they urged the seafood industry to list the source of seafood on product packaging and provide complete product labels to help consumers make choices. They have also launched a new "Seafood Selection Guide" (wwf.hk/seafood-guide), which includes a variety of seafood that Hong Kong people commonly eat, promotes the selection of environmentally friendly and sustainable species caught or farmed. They divide seafood into "Green - Recommended," Yellow - Think clearly, or "Red - Avoid." For example, we should avoid eating reef fish such as grouper, which are common in the live reef fish trade in Hong Kong. In fact, they take 5 to 10 years to reach sexual maturity, transitioning from female to male as they mature. However, they are usually caught before breeding, thus making their sex ratios unbalanced and causing reproduction more difficult.

Another fish threatened by fisheries is the shark. Sharks are important predators of the ocean, and they regulate the abundance of other animals in the food chain. This not only maintains the balance of the ecosystem, but also helps to enhance the diversity of species. Despite their important role in marine ecosystems, sharks are typically hunted for the commercial value of their fins and other body parts, such as cartilage, but their numbers are rapidly reduced due to their slow growth and low rate of fertility. Therefore, we should completely avoid food made from sharks. According to the Census and Statistics Department, the import volume of shark fins in Hong Kong continued to decline, from 2,792 tons in 2019 to 1,655 tons in 2020, a decrease of 40% year-on-year. Although the number of imports has dropped significantly, everyone should avoid eating these endangered fish and do their part to protect the ocean.

As consumers, we can protect the marine ecology by reducing or even avoiding the consumption of some endangered fish and aquatic products. If there is no demand in the market, the supply will naturally drop and the species will be protected. In fact, it is only by supporting sustainable seafood that healthy marine ecosystems can continue and our next generation can enjoy delicious seafood dishes. Whether we eat less or choose more sustainable seafood, our oceans and our future generations will thank us.
